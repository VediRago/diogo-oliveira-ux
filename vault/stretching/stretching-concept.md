# Stretching Concept

## Overview

Stretching lets the reader form an explanation, then expands that explanation later.

The first explanation stays useful. It was incomplete, not false.

## Core Pattern

Bad mystery design often works like this:

> Question → Answer → Answer was fake → New answer

Stretching uses a different pattern:

> Question → Working explanation → New evidence → Expanded explanation

The reader keeps the value of the earlier interpretation.

## Mystery Debt

Every unanswered question asks the audience for patience.

If the story only adds questions, the final answer has to carry too much weight.

Stretching gives value before asking for more uncertainty:

> Question → Reward → Larger question → Reward → Larger question

## Example: Condition to Virus

Lucreta deteriorates first. Her symptoms have no full explanation yet.

Later, Abby's world introduces a virus with overlapping symptoms. Kael appears in the same ruined world while showing similar deterioration.

A reasonable explanation forms:

> virus → symptoms → collapse

The virus is real. The symptoms match. Infection is not the full answer.

Later symptoms appear across unrelated contexts. The virus becomes one outcome inside a larger pattern.

## Difference From a Twist

A twist says:

> You were wrong.

Stretching says:

> You were right to notice that. There is more.

The reader's attention should still matter after the explanation expands.

## Failure Cases

Stretching fails when earlier evidence becomes useless.

It also fails when new information contradicts established reality, or when misdirection exists only to prolong mystery.

A stretch should make earlier scenes stronger.

## Design Rule

Earlier understanding should survive the expansion.

The reader should be able to say:

> I still understand what I saw before. I just understand more now.
