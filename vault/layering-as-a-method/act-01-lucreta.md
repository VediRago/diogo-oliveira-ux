# Act 01 — Lucreta

## Status

* Act summary
* Blueprint structure in progress
* Prose exists
* Scene blueprints created

## Act Purpose

Lucreta establishes the first major visual and emotional language of *Fragments*: ceremony, confinement, inherited duty, bodily failure, and the contrast between performed happiness and physical truth.

The act should feel beautiful but suffocating. Its world is formal, old, controlled, and ritualized. Lucreta is prepared for a wedding that should mark the beginning of a life, but the scene language makes clear that the ceremony is also a cage.

This act teaches the audience how to read the story: hands, reflections, windows, sky, delayed face reveals, small bodily failures, and silence can carry as much meaning as dialogue.

## Act Summary

Lucreta is prepared for her wedding inside a dim aristocratic room while ordinary civilian life continues outside the window. The first scene withholds her face and lets the audience discover her through posture, hands, ritual, and attention. The outside world catches her before the sky does: movement, carriages, goods, people, and daily life. When Vera pulls her away, the street disappears first, leaving only the sky as the last part of the outside Lucreta can still hold.

The mirror then becomes the center of the act. Lucreta opens her eyes and becomes fixed on the pale-mint reflection. The reflection establishes her visual identity clearly: red hair and pale green eyes, closer to teal or mint, already fading from natural life into condition. Outside voices demand that the wedding continue, while Vera tries to pull Lucreta back into the room and out of the fixation. Lucreta gives the expected replies, but physically almost nothing changes. Her body refuses to match the happiness her words perform. The cough and blood break through the ceremony, and Vera hides worry behind a practiced calm.

The act ends with Lucreta trying to continue anyway. She turns from the mirror toward the door, almost reaches the ceremony, and collapses before crossing into the life the wedding promised. Vera notices too late to prevent it. The doorway becomes a failed threshold.

## World / Design Language

* Lucreta's world is Victorian-inspired, not literal historical Victorian England.
* The visual language should feel aristocratic, ceremonial, enclosed, and socially controlled.
* Interiors should use dark wood, polished brass, heavy fabric, formal gowns, floral arrangements, mirrors, narrow etiquette, and ritual preparation.
* The room should not feel bright or free. It should feel dim, shadowed, beautiful, and controlled.
* The dim interior contrasts with the sunny life outside.
* The world outside the window should feel public, ordinary, and alive: carriages, cobbled streets, passing suits and dresses, crates of bottles, fruit baskets, parasols, hats, bags, canes, workers, movement, and daily routine.
* The outside is not pure scenery. It represents ordinary life continuing beyond Lucreta's ceremonial confinement.
* The act should avoid generic fantasy-palace imagery. It should feel like a refined aristocratic society with strict ceremony and social pressure.

## Performance Language

* Lucreta's replies often perform obedience, composure, and happiness, but physically almost nothing changes.
* Her face should remain cold, controlled, and nearly emotionless through key exchanges.
* The sarcasm comes from the mismatch between what she says and what her body refuses to express.
* Do not play her coldness as hatred toward Vera.
* Do not play her ceremonial lines as sincere joy.
* The truth appears through stillness, timing, fixation, cough, blood, missteps, and collapse.

## Vera's Function

* Vera is not an enemy force.
* Vera cares for Lucreta but expresses that care inside the rules of ceremony.
* Vera often tries to preserve the moment rather than shatter it.
* Vera tries to pull Lucreta away from the mirror fixation because the condition is consuming the wedding moment for her.
* When Vera sees worry, sadness, or fear in herself, she hides it quickly.
* Vera's practiced smile is not coldness; it is an attempt to keep Lucreta from carrying another visible burden.
* Vera's role is care constrained by ritual.

## Core Motifs

### Window

* The window connects Lucreta to ordinary life outside.
* Life outside catches her before the sky does.
* When Vera pulls Lucreta away, the street disappears first.

### Sky

* The sky is the last part of the outside that remains after the street slips from view.
* It should feel like freedom, distance, and unreachable life.
* The sky matters because it is tied to ordinary life and movement, not because it is abstract beauty.
* This is an early bridge toward Unity: each major fragment has a distinct sky moment.
* Lucreta's sky is the last remaining outside after ordinary life is taken from view.

### Mirror / Reflection

* The mirror delays Lucreta's direct face reveal.
* Reflection is tied to identity, condition, performance, and bodily failure.
* The reflection must clearly establish Lucreta's red hair and pale green eyes, closer to teal or mint, already fading.
* The eyes should read as beautiful but wrong, marking the condition through image rather than explanation.
* The condition should be understood through visual and behavioral evidence.

### Pale-Mint Eyes

* Lucreta's eyes are a visible sign of the condition.
* The eyes should not be treated only as a color detail.
* They pull attention, interrupt the wedding moment, and become part of the mirror fixation.
* This pale green / mint / teal eye language connects forward to Erit and later condition imagery.
* Lucreta and Erit sharing pale mint/teal eyes and red hair should create a visual bridge: they represent a safe self as another fragment, not a fact that needs to be explained immediately.
* The red-hair visual should help connect later Sera/Erit through color.

### Blood / Gown

* Blood breaks through the social performance.
* Wiping blood onto the gown happens by instinct before Lucreta fully realizes what she has done.
* The gown represents ceremony, purity, future, and social expectation.

## Structural Function

* Establishes the first major doorway contrast later echoed by Parents.
* Lucreta is stopped before entering the life promised by ceremony.
* Parents later begins with Philip and Abigail leaving a chapel and entering life together.
* Lucreta's act turns wedding imagery into confinement, illness, and failed transition.
