# Whole Portfolio Structure

*One of the applied methods behind [the repository's principles](../PRINCIPLES.md).*

## Principle

One question per layer. No overlap.

## Explanation

A body of work spanning multiple disciplines has two ways to fail once it grows past a handful of projects.

It flattens into one undifferentiated list, where everything competes for the same attention and nothing signals what it actually is. Or it fragments into disconnected pieces with no throughline, where a reader has no way to tell how any one piece relates to the rest.

Both failures come from the same root cause: too many jobs assigned to one place.

The fix isn't more organization. It's giving each container one job.

Each layer in this portfolio exists to answer exactly one question:

- **Layer 1** — Who is this, and where do I go from here?
- **Layer 2** — How is this work approached?
- **Layer 3** — What does that approach produce?
- **Layer 4** — What is this singular thing, on its own terms?

If a repository tries to answer more than one of these, it's in the wrong layer. That's not a style judgment. It's a diagnostic — the same way a document that tries to be both a summary and a deep dive usually fails at both.

## Application

This is information architecture applied at the scale of an entire portfolio: reducing cognitive load by giving every repository one clear purpose before asking the reader to decide where to go next.

This structure wasn't designed in advance and then filled in. It was found by running into what didn't fit.

UX was built first, deliberately, as the reference case. Narrative Design was left intentionally provisional — its structure holds for now, but it's expected to be refit once a shared Layer 2 blueprint exists, rather than solidified early and refactored later under pressure.

Layer 4 wasn't planned. It appeared because CEM exposed a gap in the architecture. CEM couldn't honestly be described as a method — it isn't a transferable process — and it couldn't be described as a product either, since it's still evolving rather than presenting itself as a finished work. The architecture changed because the work demanded it.

That's the same principle applied to the portfolio's own shape, not just the projects inside it: the system adapts to the work, not the other way around.

The result isn't a separate case study. The portfolio itself is the case study. The reader isn't being told about information architecture through an example. They're navigating one.

## Evidence

- **Layer System Blueprint** — the rules governing why each layer exists and what separates them.
- **Portfolio Layer Inventory** — the current state of every repository against those rules.
- **Ecosystem Map** — the portfolio's structure, shown rather than described.
